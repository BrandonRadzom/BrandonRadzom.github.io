In Situ Origins of Hot Jupiters

2021-Present

Image Credit: NASA/Ames/JPL-Caltech

The longest-standing mystery in exoplanets is the origin of short-period gas giants known as hot Jupiters. Conventionally, it has been thought that these giants should form like the solar system's Jupiter beyond the snow line \citep{Bodenheimer2000}, then move inward to their current orbits either through interactions with their disk \citep{Goldreich_Tremaine1979, Lin_Papa1986, Lin1996} or high-eccentricity tidal migration initiated by planet-planet scattering \citep{Rasio_Ford1996, Chatterjee2008}, Lidov-Kozai cycling \citep{Wu_2003, Fabrycky2007, Naoz2016_review}, or secular interactions \citep{Wu_2011, Petrovich2015}.

The two distant migration paradigms can each explain only a subset of the known observational properties of short-period gas giants (as reviewed by \citealt{Dawson2018}). The most significant of these, challenging both disk and high-eccentricity migration, is the observed isolation of hot Jupiters relative to warm Jupiters. Population studies have demonstrated that hot Jupiters host nearby low-mass planetary companions much less frequently than their longer period counterparts (e.g., \citealt{Steffen2012, Huang_2016, Hord_2021}). The companion rates for these two populations should be similar assuming either migration scenario, with quiescent disk migration favoring higher rates than high-eccentricity migration.

These migration-based formation scenarios, however, are solely informed by the architecture of our own solar system. Yet, the mass budget in the inner region of the minimum-mass solar nebula (MMSN, \citealt{Hayashi1981}) is severely limited, making it difficult to form sufficiently massive cores ($\sim 10 \text{ M}_{\oplus}$) for gas accretion \citep{Pollack1996}. Observations from space-based transit \citep{Borucki2010, Batalha2013, Ricker2015} and Doppler velocity \citep{Mayor2011, Howard2010, Vogt_2010} surveys, however, have revealed a widespread prevalence of close-in ``super-Earths'', suggesting that the inner regions of protoplanetary disks may be more massive than predicted from the MMSN (\citealt{Chiang_Laughlin2013, Dai_2020}, though some may have migrated to their current location via resonant chains, see \citealt{Izidoro_2017}). These compact planets often exhibit a surprising ``peas in a pod'' structure \citep{Weiss2018, Millholland_Wang_laughlin2017, Wang_2017, Goyal2022} where they share similar spacing, masses, and radii, with typical masses from a few to a few tens of Earth masses and orbital periods from a few days to months \citep{Latham2011, Lissauer2011, Lissauer2012, Fabrycky2014, Winn_Fabryky2015}.
