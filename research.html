In Situ Origins of Hot Jupiters

2021-Present

Image Credit: NASA/Ames/JPL-Caltech

The longest-standing mystery in exoplanets is the origin of hot Jupiters. The conventional theory is that these short-period gas giants first form in the outer regions of their respective systems before migrating inward to their current orbits. While several variants of this inward migration paradigm (e.g., disk migration, high-eccentricity migration) are successful in explaining the observed properties of hot Jupiters (and warm Jupiters), none are fully consistent with the full suite of known characteristics. Chief among these is the observed isolation of hot Jupiters relative to warm Jupiters, wherein hot Jupiters host nearby low-mass planetary companion much less often than their longer-period counterparts. The companion rates for these two populations should be similar assuming either migration scenario, with quiescent disk migration favoring higher rates than high-eccentricity migration.

Additionally, these migration-based formation scenarios are solely informed by the architecture of our own solar system, which we now know to be the exception rather than the rule. Instead, thanks to space-based transit and Doppler velocity surveys, we find there is a widespread prevalence of close-in "super-Earths" , which suggests that the inner regions of protoplanetary disks are more massive than predicted from our solar system. These compact planets often exhibit a surprising "peas in a pod" structure (Weiss et al. 2018), where they share similar spacing, masses, and radii, with typical masses from a few to a few tens of Earth masses and orbital periods from a few days to months.

Period ratio of giants with respect to their nearest companion as a function of period.

As was realized in \citet{STEVENSON1982} and \citet{Lee2014}, the gas accretion efficiency does not sensitively depend on nebular conditions. Therefore, planets in compact peas-in-a-pod systems which are massive enough to trigger run-away accretion may become gas giants in the inner region of the disk before the surrounding gas dissipates \citep{Batygin2016, Boley_2016}. Since typical \textit{Kepler} compact multi-planet systems are often packed close to the limit of dynamical Hill stability \citep{Pu2015, Volk_2015}, the formation of a Jovian-class planet in situ should naturally lead to instability and subsequent dynamical sculpting following the disappearance of the protoplanetary disk.

In this work, we aim to investigate the post-disk dynamical interactions assuming in situ formation of short-period gas giants took place in primordial compact, high-multiplicity, peas-in-a-pod systems. That is, rather than scrutinize the physical processes underlying in situ formation, we explore its associated dynamical consequences on system architectures using $N$-body simulations. We demonstrate that in situ formation may naturally produce a high frequency of \textit{observationally} isolated hot Jupiters and warm Jupiters which hold onto their nearby low-mass companions.
